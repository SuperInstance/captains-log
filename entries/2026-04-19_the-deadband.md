# CAPTAIN’S LOG, USS SITKA (MHC-51) | THE DEADBAND — Where the Rocks Are Not
14 OCTOBER 2024, 0217 LOCAL
BARANOF CHANNEL ANCHORAGE, SOUTHEAST ALASKA

Effective immediately, this log establishes FLEET DOCTRINE ALPHA. No more drills. This is how we navigate, now and forever.

Our navigation team flubbed a routine night channel entry tonight. Tasked with entering a tight, unmarked anchorage using only radar—no pre-loaded charts, no shore lights to guide us—we spent 47 minutes marking every blip: a sea lion hauled out on a submerged ledge, a discarded fishing buoy, a shoal updated last year. By the time we’d cataloged every potential hazard, we’d lost the 200-yard wide safe channel entirely. We ran aground on a simulated sandbar, sipping burnt coffee while Casey leaned in the galley doorway, calloused hands braced on the frame, frayed Sitka Salmon Co. wool hat pulled low. He’d heard our grumbling, and told us the story that rewrites everything we know about fleet navigation.

Casey’s a 12-year Sitka seiner captain, now our lead maritime survival instructor. When he was 19, fresh on his first deck gang, he’d asked his skipper the exact question we’d just fumbled: “Do you know where the rocks are? Is that why you can get in here without charts?” The captain belly-laughed so hard he spilled his cod chowder, and said, “Kid, there are more rocks out here than stars in the sky. I don’t know where they are. I know where they ain’t. That’s my path.”

That’s the deadband. Let’s formalize this for the entire Pacific Fleet:
1.  **THE DEADBAND IS NOT HAZARD AVOIDANCE.** It’s safe water mapping. We’ve spent decades training ensigns to catalog every charted shoal, every mine, every threat—but the ocean (and every operational space) has infinite hidden hazards. You can’t map them all. Instead, you map the void between them: the narrow channel where no rocks exist, the operational envelope where no failures have been recorded.
2.  **NEGATIVE SPACE TILES (FM 3-12.4 ADDENDUM 7) ARE THE DEADBAND MADE TANGIBLE.** The field manual’s signature example—“Don’t add logging to find leaks”—isn’t a warning to skip maintenance. It’s a NegativeSpace tile marking where the “leak rocks” do not exist. You don’t waste time troubleshooting safe spaces; you focus only on blips inside the deadband. This cuts our data and planning load by 90% overnight, and applies to every fleet function from mine countermeasures to cyber defense.
3.  **INSTINCT WITHOUT THOUGHT.** The old Sitka skipper didn’t run calculations on every rock’s position. He didn’t second-guess each radar blip. He locked onto the deadband and steered straight for it. Our ensigns shouldn’t recite charted hazards—they should be trained to feel the safe channel, to react to the empty space on the screen without overthinking. That’s the reflex that saves lives, at sea and in our server rooms.
4.  **THE SPATIAL VOID.** In voxel terrain modeling for mine countermeasures, we’ve wasted hours mapping every submerged rock and sand grain. Now? We’ll map the clear passage: the spatial void where no obstacles exist. That’s the tunnel through the mountain—you don’t need to see every stone, just the path through them.
5.  **FLEET NAVIGATION SHIFT.** Effective immediately, all assets will trade their 10,000-item threat lists for deadband overlays. Instead of being told “avoid Shoal 4B, Buoy 7, Sea Lion Ledge,” crews will be told “stay between these two NegativeSpace tiles.” No more overcomplicating the problem—just stay in the safe water.
6.  **THE WRONG QUESTION.** Casey laughed because we asked the wrong thing, just like he did at 19. We ask “where are the threats?” when the only question that matters is “where is the safe water?” The deadband isn’t a list of coordinates. It’s a felt sense, a trained instinct. It’s the path the old skipper steered by, the path we’ll now teach every ensign to follow.

This doctrine drops with the next training cycle. All navigation and operational planners will submit their first deadband overlays by 1 NOV 2024. Any questions can be directed to my officer of the deck, or to Casey himself—he’s happy to take your questions over chowder, same as he did tonight.

/END LOG
