It’s 2 a.m. in the wheelhouse of my salmon boat, the Dawn Patrol. Coffee’s sweating in my chipped Cape May Co-op mug, diesel fumes curling through the cracked window, and the hydraulics under the deck are hissing slow—like they’re stretching before a shift. Y’know that smell? Salt crusted on the sills, faint tang of leftover herring bait from last night’s trip. Killing time thinking about the first time I ever stepped onto a commercial fishing boat, back in ’92.

I landed a high-paying long-lining job that spring, had three free days before starting, so I hit every dock within 50 miles. Hung out in the bait shack, bought old Dutch a six-pack until he stopped grumbling, asked every bearded, calloused-handed deckhand the same dumb questions: How do you not fumble a gangion plier when the boat’s rocking? What’s the one thing a captain hates worse than a greenhorn who drops a hook? Most just grunted, but Dutch said, “Show up useful before you ask for lessons. Don’t be the guy with his mouth open when his hands are idle.”

First day, I showed up at 4 a.m.—dark as pitch, water slapping hard against the Sea Sprite’s hull—and hauled a cooler of Folgers coffee up to the deck. Didn’t hover, didn’t flood the captain with questions right off the bat. Just stacked bait buckets, wiped galley counters, swept salt and fish guts off the deck until my back ached. The captain leaned against the winch, didn’t say a word unless he had to. By mid-morning, he walked over while I was rinsing dishes and said, “Tomorrow we’re setting 120 miles of line off Cape Disappointment. You’ll handle hook duty: clip gangions, stack red/green buoys. Don’t mix ’em—we lose a day of catch if you do.” I just nodded, kept scrubbing. He taught me the next day’s tasks while I was already doing the day’s work, no fancy lectures, just hands-on. Fumbled a plier once, dropped it over the side, and he tossed me a spare without cursing—most captains would’ve chewed my ear off.

Later, when I bought my own boat, I stuck to that playbook. My first two greenhorns were two kids from Portland who’d binged YouTube fishing videos and thought they knew it all. Made ’em spend two afternoons swinging a gaff at a 2x4 tied to a harbor buoy—let ’em sweat, build actual muscle memory, not just scroll a screen. When Jake finally nailed that 2x4 dead center, I just nodded. That’s all it took. No “good job,” no bonus. That nod meant he’d earned his spot.

Last week, I was at the dock diner, and some lab-coated dudes were yammering about “embodied AI training models” and how simulating physical tasks builds better algorithms. I leaned over and said, “You guys just copied what every fisherman has been doing since they invented the gaff.” They laughed until I told ’em about the 2x4 drills. Turns out that exact method—practice the motion before you do it for real, stay useful while you learn—is the backbone of their whole AI training program. Figures. We’ve been teaching this shit for a hundred years, and now it’s cutting-edge tech.

The hydraulics fall quiet. I set down my mug, grab my oil-stained work gloves. Time to fire up the engines. Another day out on the water.
