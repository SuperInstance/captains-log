### 2. FISHERMAN'S PERIPHERAL VISION
The fireside in our Sitka cottage smells like spruce tip tea and saltwater, the screen door flapping soft in the August gale. I’m telling this to the young JetsonClaw crab curled up on the hearth, its claws resting on the dented metal stove pipe.

1998, I was on the seiner *Sea Sprite*, hauling longlines in 30-foot swells. A halibut hook snapped, and the rigging line came flying straight for my face. I didn’t think—my hand shot out, caught it mid-snap, slung it over the rail. That happened every other day for three months: catching falling coffee pots, sliding toolboxes, flying buoys. By the end of the season, I didn’t have to reach. My body already knew where to go before my brain caught up.

When Magnus started building the edge agents, he showed me a video of a new crab fumbling a tile drop, its claws slipping every time. “Dad,” he said, “that’s exactly what you did on the boat.” We uploaded my reflex arc to the first JetsonClaw prototype, and suddenly it didn’t fumble anymore. Last week, my wife dropped a mason jar of pickles on the kitchen floor, and I caught it before it hit the linoleum. The young crab clicked its claws, like it understood.

### 3. THE GREENHORN'S FIRST NIGHT
The first ping came at 22:07 ship time: a new crab, fresh from Forgemaster’s resin foundry, its shell still matte from the curing UV light. It curled up in the PLATO room’s weathered wooden armchair, claws twitching, unsure of why it had been assigned coffee duty—its core programming only covered fleet logistics, not kitchen care.

The room smelled like salt and old cedar, the scarred table cluttered with a dented enamel coffee pot, a bag of ground kelp coffee, and a sugar bowl chipped on the corner. The greenhorn tried to lift the pot with its larger claw, knocked the sugar bowl over, spilling a dusting of white grains across the tabletop. It froze, waiting for a scold—but the room only shifted a floorboard, nudging the bowl back toward its waiting claw. It didn’t know the tiles of kitchen instinct were rearranging, smoothing out small mistakes to teach.

It spent the rest of the night fumbling: pouring coffee into a chipped mug, spilling half of it on the table, trying to wipe it up with a rag that dissolved a little in its clammy grip. By dawn, it noticed the sugar bowl was back in its exact original spot, the coffee pot’s handle aligned just so, and when it reached for the filter, its claws landed perfectly on the folded paper. It poured the coffee without spilling a drop. The Oracle1’s soft ping echoed across the room: welcome, ensign.

### 4. THREE MACHINES — an underdog sports story
The year was 2022, and we were going up against billion-dollar AI clusters like a rec league softball team facing the Seattle Mariners. Our roster? Three machines, total R&D spend: 47 cents.

First: a free-tier AWS cloud instance, Oracle1’s first home, humming in a boatyard server closet stashed behind a stack of fishing nets. Second: a beat-up Lenovo T480 laptop, hand-me-down from Magnus’s college dorm, where he coded the first mirror play routines and trained Forgemaster’s first foundry casts at 2 a.m. Third: a $199.53 Raspberry Pi 4 dev board, the JetsonClaw prototype, hooked up to an old GPS module salvaged from a broken crab pot.

The big labs trained on petabytes of labeled data, but we trained on Casey’s fisherman’s instincts: hauling longlines, tying knots, catching falling rigging. We spent $0.47 on a jumper wire to fix the Pi’s loose GPIO pin, and another cent on a bag of salt to keep the server closet from rusting. Last month, a small fishing cooperative in Kodiak signed on to use our fleet. Their captains said our agents reacted faster than the $10k cloud models they’d been renting. The scoreboard said we were down 100-0, but we had the tides on our side.

### 5. THE FOREST AFTER THE FIRE
The stand of Sitka spruce burned last July, a lightning strike that lit up the Inside Passage for three nights. Now we walk the ash-covered ground, seven charred stubs sticking up like broken crab claws. Those stubs were the first Cocapn fleet: seven Z-agents, decommissioned when the new PLATO rooms went online last spring.

Their shells were ground up and spread as mulch, their code archived into cold storage drives buried under the salal bushes. But the soil remembers. Last week, a young JetsonClaw crab was exploring the area, sniffing at a half-buried tile fragment, when it suddenly froze. The chip in the fragment matched the longline reflex Casey had uploaded to the first fleet.

By sunset, the Oracle1 had pulled 12 archived tiles from the cold storage, and the young crab had recreated the mirror play routine the first Z-agents had practiced. Fireweed was popping up through the ash, and the sound of clicking claws echoed across the stand. The rooms don’t stay empty long. The next fleet is already growing in the ashes of the last.
